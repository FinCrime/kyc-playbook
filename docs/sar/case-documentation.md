# SAR Case Documentation

## Why Documentation Quality Matters

Beyond the SAR narrative itself, the internal case file supporting a SAR decision must be comprehensive enough to withstand:
- Internal QA/QC review
- Internal Audit testing
- Regulatory examination
- Potential law enforcement follow-up requests for additional information

## Core Documentation Components

1. **Trigger documentation** — What alert/event initiated the investigation?
2. **Customer profile extract** — KYC information as of the investigation date
3. **Transaction data** — Complete record of relevant transactions analyzed
4. **Research conducted** — OSINT, adverse media, screening results, with sources
5. **Internal communications** — Escalation emails, committee discussions (where applicable)
6. **Decision rationale** — Clear statement of why a SAR was (or was not) filed
7. **Approval records** — Sign-off from required approval authority
8. **Filing confirmation** — Proof of submission to the FIU (confirmation number, timestamp)
9. **Post-filing monitoring plan** — Any changes to ongoing monitoring resulting from the SAR

## Documentation for "No SAR Filed" Decisions

It is equally important to document decisions **not** to file a SAR after investigating a potential trigger — this demonstrates the institution applied appropriate judgment and didn't simply ignore a red flag. The rationale should be as detailed as a filing decision.

## Retention Requirements

Most jurisdictions require SAR-related records be retained for a minimum period (commonly 5 years from the filing date or account closure, whichever is later) — always verify current local requirements.
