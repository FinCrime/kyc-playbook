# SAR Narrative Writing

## Why the Narrative Is the Most Important Part

The SAR narrative is what law enforcement and FIU analysts actually read and act upon. A well-written narrative can be the difference between a SAR that contributes to a successful investigation and one that is filed and never meaningfully reviewed due to poor clarity.

## FinCEN's "Five Essential Elements" (Widely Applicable Framework)

A strong SAR narrative should answer:

1. **Who** — Who is conducting the suspicious activity?
2. **What** — What instruments/mechanisms are being used?
3. **When** — When did the activity take place (specific dates/timeframes)?
4. **Where** — Where did the activity occur (accounts, branches, geographic locations)?
5. **Why** — Why does the institution believe the activity is suspicious?

A sixth element often added: **How** — How did the activity occur (the mechanism/method)?

## Narrative Structure

### 1. Introduction
Brief summary of who the subject is and what the suspicious activity involves.

### 2. Background
Relevant account/relationship history and context.

### 3. Description of Suspicious Activity
Chronological, factual account of the specific transactions/activity, with dates and amounts.

### 4. Red Flags Identified
Clearly enumerate the specific red flags/indicators that led to suspicion.

### 5. Investigation Steps Taken
What research/verification was conducted (KYC review, OSINT, customer contact if applicable).

### 6. Conclusion
Clear statement of why the institution believes the activity is suspicious and warrants FIU attention.

## Writing Principles

:::tip Best Practices
- **Be specific.** Use exact dates, amounts, and account numbers — not "around," "approximately," or vague timeframes where specific data is available.
- **Stay factual.** Avoid speculation or unsupported conclusions; stick to what was observed and verified.
- **Use plain, clear language.** Avoid excessive jargon; the narrative should be understandable to an FIU analyst unfamiliar with your specific institution's systems.
- **Be comprehensive but concise.** Include all material facts without unnecessary padding.
- **Never include the word "alleged" excessively or hedge so much the narrative loses clarity** — state findings plainly while remaining factual.
:::

## Common Weaknesses in SAR Narratives

| Weakness | Problem | Better Approach |
|---|---|---|
| Vague language | "Customer conducted unusual transactions" | "Customer conducted 14 cash deposits totaling $94,500 between [date] and [date], each below $10,000" |
| Missing the "why" | Lists facts without stating why they're suspicious | Explicitly connects facts to a recognized typology or red flag pattern |
| Jargon-heavy | Uses internal system codes/acronyms without explanation | Plain-language description any reader can follow |
| No chronology | Facts presented out of order | Clear chronological narrative |

## Sample SAR Narrative Excerpt

> Between [Date] and [Date], [Customer Name] (Account #XXXXX) conducted 11 cash deposits totaling $98,200 across 4 different branch locations within a 9-day period. Individual deposits ranged from $8,700 to $9,950 — each below the $10,000 CTR reporting threshold. The customer's declared occupation is [occupation], with historical account activity averaging $3,000 in monthly deposits over the preceding 12 months. The sudden volume increase, the consistent sub-threshold amounts, and the use of multiple branch locations are consistent with structuring to evade CTR reporting requirements. When contacted for clarification regarding the source of funds, the customer stated the deposits were "personal savings" but was unable to provide documentation. [Institution] is filing this SAR due to reasonable grounds to suspect the deposits represent structured transactions designed to evade currency reporting requirements, in violation of 31 U.S.C. § 5324.
