# Real Estate in Money Laundering

## Overview

Real estate is one of the most commonly exploited sectors for money laundering integration. The combination of high asset values, subjective pricing, complex transaction structures, and historically weak AML oversight makes it attractive for launderers.

## Why Real Estate Is Attractive

- **High value** — Single transaction can absorb millions
- **Subjective valuation** — Easy to justify over/under pricing
- **Leverage** — Mortgage financing can blend legitimate and criminal funds
- **Ongoing income** — Rental income provides legitimate-appearing cash flow
- **Asset appreciation** — Returns on investment appear legitimate

## Common Methods

### All-Cash Purchases
Avoid mortgage scrutiny; entire purchase price comes from laundered funds.

### Shell Company Purchases
Properties purchased through anonymous shell companies, obscuring the true buyer.

### Loan-Back on Property
Criminal deposits offshore; uses it as collateral for "legitimate" mortgage onshore.

### Inflated Purchase/Sale
Property bought at below-market value from a related party; sold at market value; the profit appears as legitimate gain.

### Rental Income Mixing
Criminal proceeds declared as rental income from owned properties.

## Regulatory Response

- **FinCEN Geographic Targeting Orders (GTOs)** — US: mandatory reporting of all-cash real estate purchases above certain thresholds in targeted markets
- **EU 6AMLD** — Extended obligations to real estate agents
- **UK HMRC** — Estate agents subject to MLR 2017
- **Anti-Kleptocracy measures** — UK Economic Crime Transparency & Enforcement Act 2022 (Unexplained Wealth Orders)

## Red Flags

- All-cash purchase at above-market price
- Purchase through shell companies with no identifiable UBO
- Rapid resale with no improvements
- Purchase price does not reflect market comparable values
- Purchaser has no verifiable income consistent with purchase price
- Multiple property purchases in short succession
