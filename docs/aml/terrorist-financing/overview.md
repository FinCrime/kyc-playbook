# Terrorist Financing

## What Is Terrorist Financing?

**Terrorist financing (TF)** refers to the financial support of terrorism — providing funds to individuals, groups, or organizations that carry out or plan terrorist acts. It is distinct from money laundering in a critical way:

| | Money Laundering | Terrorist Financing |
|---|---|---|
| **Source of Funds** | Illegal (criminal proceeds) | Can be **legal or illegal** |
| **Purpose** | Disguise criminal origin | Fund terrorist activities |
| **Transaction Size** | Often large | Often **small amounts** |
| **Detection Challenge** | Paper trail | Legitimate funds, no red flags |

Terrorist financing is addressed in FATF Recommendations 5–8 and the UN Security Council Resolution 1267 (and successor resolutions).

## Key Regulatory Framework

- **UN Security Council Resolutions** — UNSCR 1267 (Taliban/Al-Qaeda), 1373 (all terrorist financing)
- **FATF Recommendations 5–8** — Criminalization, freezing, confiscation, and FI obligations
- **USA PATRIOT Act (USA)** — Section 326 (CIP), Section 311 (special measures)
- **Terrorism Act 2000 (UK)** — Sections 15–18: fundraising, use/possession, funding arrangements, money laundering
- **EU Directive 2017/541** — Combating terrorism framework

## How Terrorist Organizations Are Funded

### Legitimate Sources
- Charitable donations / Misuse of NPOs (Non-Profit Organizations)
- State sponsorship
- Legitimate business operations
- Membership fees / extortion within territory

### Illegal Sources
- Drug trafficking (Taliban, FARC)
- Kidnap for ransom
- Oil smuggling (ISIS)
- Extortion / protection rackets
- Cybercrime / fraud

## Key Red Flags for Terrorist Financing

- Transactions to/from sanctioned individuals or entities (OFAC, UN Lists)
- Payments to conflict zones with no commercial rationale
- Small, frequent cross-border transfers to high-risk jurisdictions
- Transactions involving Non-Profit Organizations in conflict-adjacent regions
- Lack of any economic or business rationale for transactions
- Customer matches or is close to a sanctioned individual

## Differences in Detection vs. AML

Terrorist financing is harder to detect because:
1. **Amounts are small** — $25,000 could finance a significant attack; this doesn't trigger large-transaction alerts
2. **Funds may be legitimate** — A donation from a legitimate salary is hard to flag without purpose intelligence
3. **Speed is critical** — Funds may be used immediately; traditional lagging AML controls may miss it

## NPO Sector Risk

Non-Profit Organizations are particularly vulnerable to TF misuse. FATF Recommendation 8 specifically addresses this. Red flags for NPO-TF misuse:
- Charity registered in a country with active conflict
- Funds transferred to regions controlled by terrorist groups
- Vague programme descriptions
- Cash-heavy operations with no verifiable beneficiaries
