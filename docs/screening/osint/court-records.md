# Court Records Research

## Overview

Court and litigation records often reveal information not captured in news media or commercial screening databases — civil disputes, bankruptcy filings, and criminal proceedings that may be highly relevant to AML risk assessment.

## Types of Relevant Court Records

| Record Type | Relevance |
|---|---|
| Criminal proceedings | Direct evidence of predicate offense involvement |
| Civil litigation (fraud, breach of contract) | May reveal undisclosed business disputes or fraud allegations |
| Bankruptcy filings | Financial distress, potential asset concealment |
| Regulatory enforcement proceedings | Administrative sanctions, license revocations |
| Family court records (in some jurisdictions, divorce proceedings) | Can reveal asset disclosures relevant to SoW verification |

## Common Court Record Databases

| Jurisdiction | Resource |
|---|---|
| United States (Federal) | PACER (Public Access to Court Electronic Records) |
| United States (State) | Varies by state court system |
| United Kingdom | Courts and Tribunals Judiciary website; Companies Court records |
| European Union | National court databases vary significantly |
| International | World Bank Listing of Ineligible Firms & Individuals (debarment) |

## Investigation Approach

1. Search by full legal name and known aliases
2. Search both as plaintiff and defendant (entity may have initiated or been subject to litigation)
3. Cross-reference associated business names
4. Review case summaries/dockets for nature of dispute
5. Where full filings are accessible, review for specific factual allegations

## Limitations

- Many court records require fee-based access or are not digitized, especially historical or lower-court records
- Sealed/confidential proceedings won't appear in public searches
- Settled civil disputes don't necessarily indicate wrongdoing — context matters significantly
- Jurisdictional variance in digitization and public access is substantial

## Interpreting Findings

:::tip Context Matters
A lawsuit alone is not evidence of wrongdoing — civil litigation is common in business and doesn't necessarily indicate criminal conduct. Assess the nature of allegations, outcome (if resolved), and pattern (isolated incident vs. recurring litigation pattern) before drawing risk conclusions.
:::

## Interview Questions

1. **What is PACER and what type of records does it provide?**
2. **Why should a single civil lawsuit not automatically be treated as a high adverse media finding?**
3. **What limitations exist in researching court records across multiple jurisdictions?**
