# Politically Exposed Person (PEP) Screening

## What Is a PEP?

A **Politically Exposed Person (PEP)** is an individual who holds, or has held, a prominent public position, creating a higher risk of involvement in bribery, corruption, or related financial crime due to the influence and access associated with their role.

FATF Recommendation 12 requires enhanced due diligence for PEP relationships due to this elevated corruption risk.

## Why PEPs Are Higher Risk

PEPs present elevated risk not because they are presumed guilty of wrongdoing, but because:
1. **Access to public funds** — Position may provide opportunity for embezzlement or misappropriation
2. **Influence over decisions** — Position may create opportunity for bribery in exchange for favorable decisions (contracts, licenses, regulatory outcomes)
3. **Network risk** — Family members and close associates may be used to layer/hide illicit proceeds
4. **Asset concealment incentive** — Greater incentive to hide wealth from public scrutiny

:::info Important Principle
PEP status is **not an accusation of wrongdoing**. It is a risk factor requiring enhanced scrutiny, not a basis for automatic relationship refusal. Many PEPs are entirely legitimate customers; the EDD process exists to distinguish risk from actual suspicion.
:::

## Categories of PEPs

→ Full detail: [PEP Categories](/docs/screening/pep/categories)

| Category | Examples |
|---|---|
| **Domestic PEP** | Senior officials in the customer's home country |
| **Foreign PEP** | Senior officials of a foreign government |
| **International Organization PEP** | Senior officials of international bodies (UN, World Bank, IMF) |
| **RCA (Relative/Close Associate)** | Family members and known close associates of PEPs |

## Common PEP Roles

- Heads of state/government
- Senior politicians (ministers, members of parliament)
- Senior government officials
- Senior judicial officials
- Senior military officials
- Senior executives of state-owned enterprises
- Senior political party officials
- Senior officials of international organizations

## PEP Status Duration

Most frameworks require PEP status (and associated EDD) to continue for a period after the individual leaves office — commonly **12 months minimum**, though many institutions apply longer periods (often indefinitely for individuals from high-corruption-risk jurisdictions) based on risk assessment, recognizing that influence and risk don't disappear immediately upon leaving a role.

## PEP Screening Process

1. **Initial screening** — Check customer name against PEP databases at onboarding
2. **Ongoing screening** — Re-screen periodically and upon list updates (customers may become PEPs after onboarding)
3. **RCA identification** — Identify family members and close associates
4. **EDD application** — Apply enhanced due diligence per [EDD framework](/docs/edd/overview)
