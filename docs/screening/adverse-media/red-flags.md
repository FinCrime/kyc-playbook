# Adverse Media Red Flags

## High-Severity Red Flags

- Conviction for fraud, money laundering, corruption, or related financial crime
- Active criminal investigation by a credible law enforcement/regulatory body
- Sanctions designation reported in media (cross-check against official lists)
- Confirmed link to organized crime or terrorist financing
- Asset freeze or confiscation order reported

## Moderate-Severity Red Flags

- Civil litigation alleging fraud or breach of fiduciary duty
- Regulatory enforcement action (fines, license suspension)
- Named in leaked document investigations (Panama Papers, Pandora Papers, Paradise Papers)
- Reported association with individuals who are themselves subject to adverse media

## Lower-Severity / Context-Dependent

- Historical allegations that were resolved without finding of wrongdoing
- Tangential mentions in broader investigative reporting
- Disputes characterized as commercial/contractual rather than criminal in nature

## Pattern Recognition

When reviewing multiple adverse media articles about an individual, look for:
- **Recurring themes** — Same type of allegation appearing across multiple unrelated sources increases credibility
- **Escalation pattern** — Allegations progressing from investigation to charges to conviction over time
- **Network effects** — Adverse media implicating associates, family members, or connected businesses

## Interview Questions

1. **What level of adverse media severity would lead you to recommend declining a relationship?**
2. **How would you weigh a single credible source versus multiple lower-credibility sources reporting the same allegation?**
3. **What does it mean if a customer is named in a "Papers" leak investigation (e.g., Panama Papers)?**
