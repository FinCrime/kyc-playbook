# Case Study: PEP Investigation

## Scenario

A private bank's onboarding team identifies a new high-net-worth applicant as a **Foreign PEP** — the customer served as a regional Trade Commissioner in Country Z for 9 years, leaving office 2 years prior to the application. Declared net worth: $8.5 million. Stated source: "family business and investment returns."

## Investigation Steps

### 1. Confirming the PEP Match and Tenure
Public government records confirm the individual's tenure as Trade Commissioner from [Year] to [Year], a role with significant influence over import/export licensing decisions in Country Z — a function with recognized corruption exposure given control over valuable commercial licenses.

### 2. Country Risk Context
Country Z scores in the bottom quartile of the Transparency International Corruption Perceptions Index, indicating elevated corruption risk context for the jurisdiction overall.

### 3. Source of Wealth Investigation
- Customer states the family operates an import/export trading business, established by the customer's father in [Year], predating the customer's public office tenure by 20+ years
- Financial statements provided show consistent revenue growth from [Year] through present, with no anomalous spikes coinciding with the customer's tenure as Trade Commissioner
- However, further investigation reveals the family business's primary trading activity is in the same product categories (textiles, agricultural goods) for which the customer's former office issued export licenses

### 4. Adverse Media and Conflict-of-Interest Research
- No direct adverse media identifying corruption allegations against the customer specifically
- However, a 2-year-old investigative journalism article (local language source) raises concerns about licensing practices during the relevant period broadly, without naming the customer specifically
- No formal investigation, charges, or sanctions identified against the customer

### 5. Business Activity Cross-Check
Export volume data suggests the family business's trading volume in licensed categories increased notably during the customer's tenure relative to the pre-tenure baseline, though a precise causal link to the customer's office cannot be established from available public information.

## Risk Assessment

This case presents a **plausible but not fully clean** picture:
- The wealth source (family business) predates public office and has documented history — a positive factor
- However, the overlap between the business's product categories and the customer's licensing authority during their tenure, combined with the volume increase during that period, creates a residual conflict-of-interest risk that cannot be fully dismissed
- No specific adverse finding exists, but the broader context (country corruption risk + sectoral overlap + volume increase coinciding with tenure) warrants caution

## Decision and Rationale

**Recommendation:** Approve with enhanced conditions rather than outright decline, given the absence of any specific adverse finding against the individual:
- PEP designation maintained with heightened (semi-annual rather than annual) review frequency
- Enhanced transaction monitoring with lower alert thresholds
- Request for periodic (not just one-time) source of wealth updates given the ongoing family business activity
- Senior management approval explicitly noting the residual conflict-of-interest consideration in the file

## Key Takeaways

1. **PEP investigation requires synthesizing multiple weak signals**, not just searching for a single confirmed red flag — the absence of a "smoking gun" doesn't mean the analysis is complete
2. **Business activity overlapping with a PEP's former regulatory/licensing authority** is a specific and important pattern to examine, beyond generic source of wealth verification
3. **A nuanced, documented "approve with conditions" outcome** is often more appropriate than a binary approve/decline when risk is real but not conclusively disqualifying
